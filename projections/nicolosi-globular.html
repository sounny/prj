History

Abū Rayḥān Muḥammad ibn Aḥmad Al-Bīrūnī (973–1048 CE), the foremost scholar of the Islamic Golden Age, invented the first recorded globular projection for use in celestial maps around the year 1000 CE. Al-Bīrūnī's projection represented a hemisphere within a circle, a form that would prove enduringly practical for world cartography.

Centuries later, as Europe entered its Age of Discovery, demand for world maps increased rapidly, prompting experimentation with diverse projection methods. Globular projections received early attention, with contributions from Roger Bacon in the 13th century, Petrus Apianus in the 16th century, and French Jesuit priest Georges Fournier, also in the 16th century.

In 1660, Giovanni Battista Nicolosi, a Sicilian chaplain working in Rome, independently reinvented al-Bīrūnī's projection as a modification of Fournier's first projection. It is unlikely Nicolosi was aware of al-Bīrūnī's prior work; Nicolosi's name is the one conventionally associated with the projection in Western cartographic literature. Nicolosi published a set of maps on the projection — one of the world in two hemispheres, and one each for the five continents then recognized.

Maps using the Nicolosi projection appeared occasionally over the ensuing centuries, becoming relatively common in the 19th century as the stereographic projection fell out of common use for hemispheric world maps. Use continued into the early 20th century. The projection is rarely employed today.

Description & Construction

The Nicolosi globular projection is a polyconic map projection that displays a single hemisphere within a bounding circle. As a compromise projection, it preserves no single geometric property — it is neither conformal, equal-area, nor equidistant — but achieves a visually balanced distribution of distortions across the hemisphere.

The construction is geometric and can be executed with compasses and straightedge:

  • The poles are placed at the top and bottom of the bounding circle.
  • The central meridian is drawn as a straight vertical diameter.
  • The equator is drawn as a straight horizontal diameter.
  • Each remaining meridian is a circular arc through both poles, spaced equally along the equator.
  • Each remaining parallel is a circular arc from the left edge through the central meridian to the right edge, spaced equally along both the perimeter and the central meridian.

A hemisphere shown with the Nicolosi projection closely resembles one shown with the azimuthal equidistant projection centered on the same point. In both, meridians are equally spaced along the equator, and parallels are equally spaced along the central meridian and the perimeter.

Mathematical Formulae

Nicolosi developed the projection as a drafting technique. The mathematical translation yields the following forward projection equations, where φ is latitude, λ is longitude, λ₀ is the central longitude, and R is the sphere radius:

b = π / (2(λ−λ₀)) − 2(λ−λ₀) / π c = 2φ / π d = (1 − c²) / (sin φ − c) M = (b·sin φ / d − b/2) / (1 + b²/d²) N = (d²·sin φ / b² + d/2) / (1 + d²/b²) x = (π/2)·R · (M ± √(M² + cos²φ / (1 + b²/d²))) y = (π/2)·R · (N ± √(N² − (d²/b²·sin²φ + d·sin φ − 1) / (1 + d²/b²)))

The ± in the x formula takes the sign of (λ − λ₀). The ± in the y formula takes the opposite sign of φ.

Special Cases

The general formulae are undefined under certain conditions. Use the following instead:

When λ − λ₀ = 0 (central meridian): x = 0 y = R·φ When φ = 0 (equator): x = R·(λ−λ₀) y = 0 When |λ − λ₀| = π/2 (boundary meridian): x = R·(λ−λ₀)·cos φ y = (π/2)·R·sin φ When |φ| = π/2 (poles): x = 0 y = R·φ
